Maxwell's Equations and Lorentz Force in Geometric Algebra

In this document, we explore the transformation of Maxwell’s equations and the Lorentz force into the framework of geometric algebra (GA), a powerful mathematical system that unifies scalars, vectors, and bivectors into a single, elegant formalism. We’ll derive these equations step-by-step, explain the reasoning behind the transformations, and discuss their physical and mathematical meanings. Let’s dive in!

Maxwell’s Equations in Geometric Algebra

Standard Form in Vector Calculus

First, recall Maxwell’s equations in their traditional vector calculus form (in SI units):

  1. Gauss’s Law (electric): \(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{E} = \frac{\rho}{\varepsilon_0}\)
    (Divergence of the electric field is proportional to charge density.)
  2. Gauss’s Law (magnetic): \(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} = 0\)
    (Magnetic field has no divergence; no magnetic monopoles.)
  3. Faraday’s Law: \(\nabla \times \mathbf{E} = -\frac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t}\)
    (A changing magnetic field induces an electric field.)
  4. Ampère-Maxwell Law: \(\nabla \times \mathbf{B} = \mu_0 \mathbf{J} + \mu_0 \varepsilon_0 \frac{\partial \mathbf{E}}{\partial t}\)
    (Currents and changing electric fields induce magnetic fields.)

Here, \(\mathbf{E}\) is the electric field, \(\mathbf{B}\) is the magnetic field, \(\rho\) is charge density, \(\mathbf{J}\) is current density, \(\varepsilon_0\) is the permittivity of free space, and \(\mu_0\) is the permeability of free space.

Why Geometric Algebra?

Vector calculus splits the electromagnetic field into separate \(\mathbf{E}\) and \(\mathbf{B}\) components and uses distinct operators (\(\nabla \cdot\) and \(\nabla \times\)). Geometric algebra offers:

  • Combination of \(\mathbf{E}\) and \(\mathbf{B}\) into a single bivector field.
  • A unified geometric product replacing divergence and curl.
  • A coordinate-free, relativistic-friendly framework.

GA uses multivectors and the geometric product (dot + wedge), simplifying Maxwell’s equations into one compact form.

Derivation in Geometric Algebra

Step 1: Define the Electromagnetic Field

In GA, we work in 4D spacetime with basis vectors \(\{\gamma_0, \gamma_1, \gamma_2, \gamma_3\}\), where \(\gamma_0\) is timelike and \(\gamma_1, \gamma_2, \gamma_3\) are spatial, satisfying:

  • \(\gamma_0^2 = 1\), \(\gamma_1^2 = \gamma_2^2 = \gamma_3^2 = -1\) (Minkowski metric).
  • \(\gamma_a \gamma_b = -\gamma_b \gamma_a\) for \(a \neq b\).

The electromagnetic field is a bivector \(F\):

\[ F = \mathbf{E} + I c \mathbf{B} \]

where:

  • \(\mathbf{E} = E_1 \gamma_1 \gamma_0 + E_2 \gamma_2 \gamma_0 + E_3 \gamma_3 \gamma_0\) (electric field as a spacetime bivector),
  • \(\mathbf{B} = B_1 \gamma_2 \gamma_3 + B_2 \gamma_3 \gamma_1 + B_3 \gamma_1 \gamma_2\) (magnetic field as a spatial bivector),
  • \(I = \gamma_0 \gamma_1 \gamma_2 \gamma_3\) (pseudoscalar),
  • \(c\) is the speed of light.

Step 2: Spacetime Gradient

The differential operator is \(\nabla = \gamma^\mu \partial_\mu\), where \(\partial_\mu = \frac{\partial}{\partial x^\mu}\) and \(x^\mu = (ct, x, y, z)\). In natural units (\(c = 1\)): \(\nabla = \gamma_0 \frac{\partial}{\partial t} + \gamma_1 \partial_1 + \gamma_2 \partial_2 + \gamma_3 \partial_3\).

Step 3: Source Terms

The 4-current is \(J = \rho \gamma_0 - J_1 \gamma_1 - J_2 \gamma_2 - J_3 \gamma_3\), a spacetime vector.

Step 4: Unified Maxwell Equation

In GA, Maxwell’s equations become:

\[ \nabla F = \frac{J}{\varepsilon_0 c} \]

The geometric product \(\nabla F = \nabla \cdot F + \nabla \wedge F\):

  • Dot product: \(\nabla \cdot F = \nabla \cdot \mathbf{E} + I \nabla \cdot \mathbf{B}\),
  • Wedge product: \(\nabla \wedge F = (\nabla \times \mathbf{E} + \frac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t}) + I c (\nabla \times \mathbf{B} - \frac{\partial \mathbf{E}}{\partial t})\).

Equating to \(\frac{J}{\varepsilon_0 c}\), we recover:

  • Scalar: \(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{E} = \frac{\rho}{\varepsilon_0}\),
  • Trivector: \(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} = 0\),
  • Bivector: \(\nabla \times \mathbf{E} = -\frac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t}\), \(\nabla \times \mathbf{B} = \mu_0 \mathbf{J} + \mu_0 \varepsilon_0 \frac{\partial \mathbf{E}}{\partial t}\) (with \(c^2 = \frac{1}{\mu_0 \varepsilon_0}\)).

Reasoning and Meaning

  • Unification: One equation replaces four, showing \(\mathbf{E}\) and \(\mathbf{B}\) as parts of \(F\).
  • Geometry: \(F\) represents oriented planes in spacetime.
  • Relativity: The form is Lorentz-invariant.

Including the Lorentz Force

Standard Lorentz Force

The Lorentz force on a charge \(q\) with velocity \(\mathbf{v}\) is:

\[ \mathbf{f} = q (\mathbf{E} + \mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{B}) \]

This is the 3D force, but we need its 4D relativistic form.

Derivation in Geometric Algebra

Step 1: 4D Lorentz Force

The 4-force is \(\frac{dp}{d\tau} = q \frac{F \cdot u}{c}\), where \(u = \gamma (c, \mathbf{v})\) is the 4-velocity (\(\gamma = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - v^2/c^2}}\)).

Step 2: Compute \(F \cdot u\)

With \(F = \mathbf{E} + I c \mathbf{B}\) and \(u = \gamma_0 c \gamma + \gamma_i v_i \gamma\):

  • Electric part: \(\mathbf{E} \cdot u \approx q \mathbf{E}\) (spatial),
  • Magnetic part: \(I c \mathbf{B} \cdot u\) yields \(\mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{B}\) terms.

The spatial component is:

\[ \mathbf{f} = q (\mathbf{E} + \mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{B}) \]

Step 3: Integration with Maxwell

Combine:

  • Field: \(\nabla F = \frac{J}{\varepsilon_0 c}\),
  • Force: \(\frac{dp}{d\tau} = q \frac{F \cdot u}{c}\).

Reasoning and Meaning

  • Unification: Electric and magnetic forces unify in \(F \cdot u\).
  • Relativity: The 4D form is relativistic.
  • Geometry: Force as a bivector-rotation effect.

Conclusion

Geometric algebra transforms Maxwell’s equations into \(\nabla F = \frac{J}{\varepsilon_0 c}\) and extends to the Lorentz force as \(\frac{dp}{d\tau} = q \frac{F \cdot u}{c}\). This unified framework reveals the deep geometric and relativistic nature of electromagnetism, simplifying both field dynamics and particle interactions.
